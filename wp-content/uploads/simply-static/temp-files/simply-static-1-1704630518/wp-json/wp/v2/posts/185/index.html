The\u00a0Lockheed SR-71<\/strong>\u00a0“Blackbird<\/strong>” is a retired\u00a0long-range<\/a>, high-altitude,\u00a0Mach<\/a>\u00a03+\u00a0strategic<\/a>\u00a0reconnaissance aircraft<\/a>\u00a0developed and manufactured by the American aerospace company\u00a0Lockheed Corporation<\/a>.\u00a0The SR-71 has several nicknames, including “Blackbird<\/a>” and “Habu<\/a>“.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The SR-71 was developed as a\u00a0black project<\/a>\u00a0from the\u00a0Lockheed A-12<\/a>\u00a0reconnaissance aircraft during the 1960s by Lockheed’s\u00a0Skunk Works<\/a>\u00a0division. American aerospace engineer\u00a0Clarence “Kelly” Johnson<\/a>\u00a0was responsible for many of the aircraft’s innovative concepts.\u00a0The shape of the SR-71 was based on that of the Lockheed A-12, which was one of the first aircraft to be designed with a reduced\u00a0radar cross-section<\/a>. Initially, a\u00a0bomber<\/a>\u00a0variant of the A-12 was requested by\u00a0Curtis LeMay<\/a>, before the program was focused solely on reconnaissance. The SR-71 was longer and heavier than the A-12, allowing it to hold more fuel as well as a two-seat cockpit. The SR-71’s existence was revealed to the public in July 1964; it entered service in the\u00a0United States Air Force<\/a>\u00a0(USAF) in January 1966.\u00a0In 1989, the USAF retired the SR-71, largely for political reasons;\u00a0several were briefly reactivated during the 1990s before their second retirement in 1998.\u00a0NASA<\/a>\u00a0was the final operator of the Blackbird, using it as a research platform; it was retired again in 1999.<\/p>\n\n\n
